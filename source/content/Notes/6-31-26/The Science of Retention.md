# The Learning Process
## Encoding
The first step of the learning process is encoding. This is where the brain is converting what it perceives (from sensory inputs) into mental representations of what you observe; these are called "memory traces", and they don't last for very long. You need to take action to retain information after encoding.

> Nick once had a dog named Wrinkles.

Short-term memory sucks. Unless you're actually making sense of what you're learning about, you will forget.
## Consolidation
New learning is labile; it's not fully formed, and it's easily altered. We're not taking photographs in our brain. This is where consolidation comes in; it's the stabilization of memory traces over time. You want to practice replaying information, giving meaning, and making connections to other knowledge. 

A common misconception is that knowledge is best accrued by not memorizing too much, but the opposite is actually true. The more connections you can make, the more robust your knowledge will be.

Imagine you're in a forest and you need to get from Point A to Point B. Although the first trip might be difficult (hacking through bushes with your machete and such), the subsequent trips gradually become easier. This is what consolidation is.
## Retrieval
Even once we encode and consolidate knowledge, we still need to associate it with a set of cues to support future recall. Retrieval is actually the primary limit on human memory, not consolidation.

You can hypothetically store multiple lifetimes worth of information. It's functionally unlimited. But using the information is the problem.
### The Testing Effect
The Testing Effect strongly suggests that testing yourself is the best way to learn. Learning not just what, but how and when to use information is key.

> Humans lose about 70% of what they've heard or read in short order (Ebbinghaus, 1964).

A landmark study found that forgetting grew the longer a first test was delayed, but once a student took a test, forgetting nearly stopped.
### Spaced Practice
We know that practice is far more effective when it's spaced out over time (Cepeda, 2006). Massed practice brings rapid gains, but rapid loss follows once practice ends (Bloom, 1981). 
#### Medical Student Example
If you had to make an effective study program for medical students two months before a test, how would you do it?
* Consistently practice the information; every day/other day
* Prioritize new information over old information, but review old information four days after it was practiced
* Review oldest information two weeks after the last time it was practiced

The actual best method was **testing oneself**. Just knowing information in debate is not the same as deploying it in a debate round. If you don't use it, you will lose it. Otherwise, you'll fall into the illusion of mastery.

Have you ever sat down before a test to do a practice test with the answer key next to you, and every time you didn't know an answer on the practice test, you took a *quick* little peek at the answer key?

"Oh, yeah, I totally would have gotten that question right." 

Nope.

You have to plan out in advance that you're going to study for a long period of time.
### Interleaving
Interleaved practice mixes two or more skills within a single practice session. Instead of doing two hours of history and two hours of math, doing one hour of history, one hour of math, another hour of history, and another hour of math is vastly superior. This is not only because it acts like a mini spaced repetition method, but it also teaches you when to apply the knowledge you're using.

> Interleaved practice results in significantly improved long-term student performance (Kerr & Booth, 1978; Rohrer & Taylor, 2007; Goode, Geraci & Roediger, 2008).

However, learning from interleaved practice feels far slower, causing students to opt for non-interleaved practice *even* when they can see the results of interleaving.
### Hard Learning is Good Learning
> The easier a skill is for you to retrieve, the less your retrieval practice will benefit your retention of it.
> - Brown, Roediger III, and McDaniel

Difficulty trades off against the illusion of mastery, since difficult learning feels less productive even though it works better. Literally think of your brain as a muscle; if you're not struggling to build connections, you're not learning.
