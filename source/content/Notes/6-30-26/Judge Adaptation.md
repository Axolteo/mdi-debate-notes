Appealing to the judge doesn't mean fine-tuning yourself to what the judge wants; it's about *generally* appealing to the judge. 
1. Sound persuasive. It doesn't matter if you're following their paradigm if you don't sound persuasive.
2. Be as *generally* likeable as possible, even out of round.
3. Form relationships with judges and prioritize RFDs. If you get a repeat judge (or your friend has been judged by them, to a lesser extent), look back at the RFD and see what to improve on.
4. Sounding like you're **winning** makes you a **winner**
	1. A pretty speaking voice, confidence, and a **good ballot story** ensures consistent success
	2. You want someone to watch you and think, "oh, they're good"
# Understand Persuasion
* Understand logos/ethos/pathos
	* Make understandable arguments
	* Be emotionally appealing
	* Be a credible debater
* Get to the truth of the resolution
* **Ballot stories**
* Practice speaking
	* Rhetoric, word choice, diction, tempo, inflection, etc.
* Be whoever judges want to vote for
* It doesn't matter how flowery your language is if the judge can't follow
# AFF Strategy
The 2AR wins debates, but the 1AR loses them.
## 2AR
The 2AR should be similar in most debates; you'll be on the side of truth (mostly). Tell a story, give a big picture, and make the round clear. Don't be afraid to say something if you think it's a new argument.

Use rhetoric like "it's *ridiculous* to think any way to the contrary".
## 1AR
The 1AR is the bridge to the 2AR; this one needs the most practice. Extensions should add new information and characterize the story of the contention.

Say you have the living wage topic and the first contention is about homelessness, explaining how a living wage solves homelessness. Adding some sort of statistic to the extension (33% of Americans are homeless) can create a bigger impact. 

Every single NEG argument should have a turn on it in the 1AR; just focus on making as many paths to the ballot. 

Quickly extend your framework and the "this matters under their criterion as well" argument to give you more paths to the ballot. 
## AC
Write thinking forwards and backwards, and *write your own cases!!* You'll understand it so much better. Have an AC that is built to respond to the negative.
# NEG Strategy
The NC should bury the 1AR, and the NR should clean up the mess.
## NR
Clean up the debate for the judge and give KVIs. Call the AFF abhorrent. If the 2AR can get up and say you didn't respond to something, call it wraps. You need to make the 2AR sound whiny and stupid; make them go for the least persuasive option possible.

## NC
Have LOTS of blocks; short Phil NCs are always good at contesting the AFF framework as well. You need to turn every AFF contention and set up burdens for the AFF in the short time they have for the 1AR. 

"If you can't understand it, don't vote on it" is a really nice line. "The 2AR needs to give you a comprehensible way to give you moral judgements for this criterion; if it can't do that, you can't vote on it".
# Mindset
There is *no such thing as objectivity in debate*. The point of the round is to get the ballot.

There is *no such thing as a screw*. If you have lost the ballot, you have lost the round. Unless there is a judge who is actively plotting against you personally, **you have not been screwed.** It's your job to make your judge's implicit biases flow in your favor. 

Thinking about a screw makes you a worse debater; it will...
1. Release you from responsibility. You don't take the energy to be better next time; losing a round is always for a reason.
2. Make you take the judge less seriously. It makes it harder for you to adapt the judge.
3. Let you underestimate your opponent.
# Materials
## Anecdotes
Anecdotes are SUPER persuasive. Brainstorm an anecdote for each card in your case, then quiz yourself. Yes, quiz yourself. Treat it like studying. When brainstorming about a topic, you should also think about a broad-scale anecdote that explain what you're talking about.

Also memorize relevant examples. You need to know at least something about real-world examples relating to the topic, so that even if you don't have a card on it, you can at least explain mechanisms/provide more evidence for your side. The same applies for counter-examples.

Aphorisms are short statements that are basically mic drops. You should have them locked and loaded; there isn't really a good way to prepare these, just have a lot of practice debates. Have at least some *memorable* lines for the judge prepared.

"The AFF is playing with matches in a room full of dynamite"
"The oppressed don't owe you a blueprint"

Practicing extemporaneous speech helps you speak *to* the judge instead of just reading off a doc; it really appeals to judges as well.
### Anecdote Practice
*For a card about how recognizing the intrinsic value of nature spills over to prop up vital environmental movements:*

In 1973, in response to logging in the Himalayas, protesters who recognized the value of trees to local ecosystems in mitigating monsoon impacts and agricultural yields began hugging trees to spread awareness of this value. This snowballed across India, eventually creating the "Save the Himalayas" movement and setting the foundation for future environmental progress in India.

*For a card about how litigation-centric approaches to the environmental crisis like the RoN are uniquely effective:*

In 2024, Peru granted Maranon River legal personhood and rights, giving it the right to flow, maintain natural cycles, be restored, and be protected from contamination. The court ordered the state-owned oil company, Petroperú, to update its environmental management instruments to mitigate the impacts of pipeline operations.
